01 — The two levers

The Fed controls liquidity with two dials

Learn to read both and you can anticipate the market's direction. One sets the price of money; the other sets its quantity.

FEDFUNDS

The policy rate

The base price of money — every loan, bond, and mortgage is priced off it.

LOW ↓Cheap money → more liquidity (cuts in 2008, 2020)
HIGH ↑Expensive money → less liquidity (hikes in 2004, 2022)
WALCL

The balance sheet

The assets the Fed holds — mostly Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities.

QE ↑Buying bonds pushes cash in → more liquidity
QT ↓Selling / runoff drains cash → less liquidity

Most liquid market = low rate + rising balance sheet.  Least liquid = high rate + shrinking balance sheet.

04 — The valuation toolkit

How to actually price a company

Two multiples, one leverage gauge, and the mechanism that ties every valuation back to interest rates.

P/E ratio

Market cap ÷ Net income

Years to earn back what you paid. Low P/E isn't automatically cheap — future earnings beat trailing ones. Always use forward P/E, and validate the multiple against peer growth via regression (trust it only at R² > 0.8).

EV/EBITDA

Enterprise Value ÷ EBITDA

Values the whole business regardless of capital structure — like quoting a house at its full price, not the equity after the mortgage. Best for leveraged, heavy-asset firms and money-losers with real cash flow (early Tesla). Not for banks.

Debt / EBITDA

Total debt ÷ EBITDA

Roughly how many years of cash flow to repay the debt — the leverage gauge. Matters more every cycle: a company's ability to refinance swings hard with the Fed's rate.

WACC → the rate bridge

Lower rates → lower WACC → higher value

This is why the framework is Fed-centric. Lower rates cut the discount rate on future cash flows (lifting present value and multiples) and push capital out of bonds into stocks.

07 — The differentiator

Knowledge without temperament fails

The most-stressed lesson: plenty of people with 15+ years in banking, PE, and hedge funds still lose money personally. The variable isn't knowledge — it's mindset.

1

Have your own principle — and stick to it

Every strategy works for someone; none works for everyone. Match the approach to your character. His: mid/long-term, valuation-driven, macro-timed.

2

Don't get swayed by noise

The moment you chase someone else's win, you become a follower dragged into the game — not a decision-maker. (See his "lost $100k in a day" story.)

3

Review your principle regularly

Markets evolve; test your strategy honestly and stay nimble — but adapt with your own logic, never by copying others.

4

Remove emotion after you invest

Treat invested money as someone else's. Red flag: checking your balance more than three times a day on a long-term hold.

5

Don't tell others what you bought

Sharing makes you anxious about being right in front of others, which erodes discipline. Keep your thesis to yourself.
